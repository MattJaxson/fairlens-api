Community-Defined Fairness (CDF v1.0)

AI fairness research has produced at least 21 competing mathematical definitions of fairness (Verma & Rubin, 2018). They are provably mutually exclusive — no algorithm can satisfy calibration, equalized odds, and statistical parity simultaneously (Chouldechova, 2017; Kleinberg et al., 2016). The field has spent a decade stuck in a theoretical loop because it treats fairness as a mathematical property to be optimized rather than a social contract to be negotiated. The threshold that determines whether an AI system is “fair enough” still comes from a federal regulation written in 1978 or a vendor’s legal team. No one asks the community.

Project Libra resolves this impasse by moving the locus of fairness definition from the researcher’s desk to the community table. CDF v1.0 does not pick a mathematical definition — it asks the affected community to set the threshold (θ) that determines when disparate impact is unacceptable to them. This is adversarial debiasing with democratic input: Fairlearn’s ExponentiatedGradient constrained by community-sourced parameters, not corporate defaults. Every decision enters the Cryptographic Provenance Ledger — SHA-256 hash-chained and tamper-evident. Every community conversation is translated into quantitative parameters through the Q2Q Translation Matrix, closing the gap between what people say and what the algorithm enforces.

If artificial general intelligence emerges from systems trained on human-generated data and human-defined objectives, then the quality of that emergence depends on whether we built the governance infrastructure before the capability arrived. Libra is that infrastructure — a protocol for encoding collective human values into the mathematical constraints that bound AI behavior. Not top-down corporate ethics. Not regulatory minimums. Localized, documented, cryptographically proven community consensus. We are building the governance layer that benevolent AI requires — one community session at a time, one signed standard at a time, one provenance record at a time.

How We Got Here
1978
EEOC Uniform Guidelines establish the “four-fifths rule” — if a selection rate for a protected group is less than 80% of the highest group, it constitutes adverse impact. This becomes the default fairness threshold for decades.
2018
The impossibility results. Chouldechova and Kleinberg et al. prove that competing fairness definitions are mathematically incompatible. The field fragments. Researchers propose definitions. None of them ask the affected community which one matters.
2021–2024
Regulation wave: NYC Local Law 144 mandates bias audits for hiring AI. Colorado passes the AI Act. Michigan proposes HB 4668. All use vendor-selected thresholds — the community still has no formal voice.
2026
CDF v1.0 + Cryptographic Provenance Ledger. Project Libra launches the first cryptographically verifiable community fairness standard. Every threshold decision is SHA-256 hash-chained with full audit trail. The Q2Q Translation Matrix documents exactly how community deliberation becomes quantitative parameters.

What CDF v1.0 Records
THE STANDARD
  • Fairness threshold (e.g., 0.90 instead of EEOC 0.80)
  • Priority groups identified by the community
  • Definition of “favorable outcome”
  • Acceptable metric (DI ratio, statistical parity, etc.)
  • Q2Q Translation Matrix — documented mapping from community deliberation to quantitative parameters
THE PROVENANCE
  • Who participated (count, not names)
  • When the session was held
  • Input protocol (session, survey, interview)
  • Facilitator
  • Location
  • Expiry date (24-month staleness window)
  • Cryptographic hash (SHA-256) — tamper-evident chain linking each decision to its predecessor
First Pilot — District 3, Detroit

The first community in America to formally govern a municipal AI system

Detroit’s District 3 operates “Emily,” an AI voice agent that handles resident service calls. Emily processes calls from a majority-Black district — and AI speech recognition has documented failure rates with Black American English.

Libra’s first pilot puts 10–15 District 3 residents in a room to set the fairness standard for Emily. Not the vendor’s lawyers. Not a federal regulation from 1978. The people Emily actually serves.

The result: a signed District 3 Community Fairness Standard — one page, full cryptographic provenance, presentable at city council, and enforceable against the vendor. Six-month review built in. Every parameter traceable through the Q2Q Translation Matrix back to the community’s own words.

Why This Matters

The impossibility theorems are not a dead end — they are an invitation. If no single mathematical definition of fairness can satisfy all desiderata simultaneously, then the choice of which definition to prioritize is inherently a value judgment. Value judgments belong to the people affected, not to the engineers who built the system.

Regulation is necessary but insufficient. NYC LL144 requires audits but lets vendors pick the threshold. Colorado requires impact assessments but doesn’t specify who defines “acceptable.” CDF fills the gap between compliance and accountability with cryptographic proof that the standard came from the community, not a boardroom.

We are building the governance layer that benevolent AI requires. If we want AI systems that serve humanity broadly and equitably, we cannot wait until the capability arrives to build the governance. The infrastructure for encoding collective human values into machine constraints must exist first. Libra is that infrastructure — localized, documented, cryptographically proven, and owned by the communities it serves.
