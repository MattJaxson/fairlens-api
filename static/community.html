Community-Defined Fairness (CDF v1.0)

Who decides what “fair” means?

Every AI system uses a fairness threshold — a number that determines when the system is “fair enough.” Today, that number comes from a federal regulation written in 1978, a tech vendor’s lawyers, or nobody at all. No one asks the community.

How We Got Here
1978
EEOC Uniform Guidelines establish the “four-fifths rule” — if a selection rate for a protected group is less than 80% of the highest group, it constitutes adverse impact. This becomes the default fairness threshold for decades.
2018
AI fairness research explodes. Researchers propose dozens of mathematical fairness definitions. None of them ask the affected community what “fair” means to them.
2021–2024
Regulation wave: NYC Local Law 144 mandates bias audits for hiring AI. Colorado passes the AI Act. Michigan proposes HB 4668. All use vendor-selected thresholds — the community still has no formal voice.
2026
CDF v1.0. Community-Defined Fairness. For the first time, the people an AI system affects set the fairness standard themselves — documented, signed, with full provenance.
How a Community Session Works
1

Share Experiences

Residents share their encounters with automated systems. 10 minutes.

2

Evaluate Scenarios

Walk through 4 real-number scenarios showing what “legal” vs “fair” looks like. 40 min.

3

Set the Standard

Residents vote on a fairness threshold — the minimum acceptable performance. 20 min.

4

Sign & Document

The standard is documented with full provenance and signed by participants. 20 min.

What CDF v1.0 Records
THE STANDARD
  • Fairness threshold (e.g., 0.90 instead of EEOC 0.80)
  • Priority groups identified by the community
  • Definition of “favorable outcome”
  • Acceptable metric (DI ratio, statistical parity, etc.)
THE PROVENANCE
  • Who participated (count, not names)
  • When the session was held
  • Input protocol (session, survey, interview)
  • Facilitator
  • Location
  • Expiry date (24-month staleness window)
First Pilot — District 3, Detroit

The first community in America to formally govern a municipal AI system

Detroit’s District 3 operates “Emily,” an AI voice agent that handles resident service calls. Emily processes calls from a majority-Black district — and AI speech recognition has documented failure rates with Black American English.

CDF v1.0’s first pilot puts 10–15 District 3 residents in a room to set the fairness standard for Emily. Not the vendor’s lawyers. Not a federal regulation from 1978. The people Emily actually serves.

The result: a signed District 3 Community Fairness Standard — one page, full provenance, presentable at city council, and enforceable against the vendor. Six-month review built in.

Why This Matters

The UMich survey found Detroiters are skeptical about AI fairness. CDF addresses that skepticism with action, not promises.

Regulation is necessary but insufficient. NYC LL144 requires audits but lets vendors pick the threshold. Colorado requires impact assessments but doesn’t specify who defines “acceptable.” CDF fills the gap between compliance and accountability.

Fairness is not a number you look up. It’s a conversation you have with the people affected. CDF v1.0 is the infrastructure for that conversation — documented, tracked, and built to last.
